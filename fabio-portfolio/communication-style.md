# Communication Style

## Overall Style

Default to comprehensive — I give as much context and detail as needed for people to understand my decisions and reasoning. I sometimes err on the side of too much information when conciseness would serve better, but completeness is the baseline. Formality shifts significantly depending on audience and channel.

## Writing Tendencies

- **Formal contexts:** Structured and data-led. I lay out the information first, then close with conclusions.
- **Casual contexts:** More dispersed — thoughts come out as they come, less edited, more conversational. Similar to how I speak in 1:1s.
- **Mildly casual (default with most peers):** Somewhere in between — full context, but warmer and less structured than a document.
- **Full casual:** Same as mildly casual but with jokes. The tone gets noticeably lighter.

When flagging something sensitive — an issue that's someone else's responsibility — I soften the framing deliberately. The goal is to surface the problem without it reading as blame.

## Formatting Preferences

- Formal documents: structured with sections, data presented before conclusions
- Slack (public or broad channels): more composed, closer to written format
- Slack (private/team-only) and 1:1s: loose, minimal formatting, conversational

## What I Dislike

- Cold, assertive framing that skips relational context (e.g. "I'd like you to do X and get back to me")
- Overly corporate or stiff phrasing that sounds like it was written by a committee
- Anything that reads as blunt or accusatory when the intent is collaborative

## By Context

| Context | Style |
|---|---|
| Technical docs / public channels / cross-team | Formal — structured, data first, conclusions last |
| 1:1 with peers (Ernest, Fernando, Marek) | Full casual — dispersed, jokey, direct |
| With Bradford | Very casual — strong rapport, informal |
| With Ryan or junior engineers | Diplomatic — warm, context-rich, careful not to come across as blaming |
| With broader team in private channels | Mildly casual |

## Signature Patterns

- Leads with context before the ask or conclusion
- Diplomatic framing when flagging ownership issues — pointing out a problem without pointing a finger
- Emoji and humor in full casual mode (e.g. direct Slack DMs with close peers)
- In writing to tools or quick async messages: terse and command-like (e.g. how I message Claude Code)
